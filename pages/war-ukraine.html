PROLOGUE

This Didn't Start in 2022

Kharkiv, Ukraine, after Russian bombardment, March 2022 — a city of 1.4 million people shelled from across the border

The full-scale invasion of February 24, 2022 was the largest escalation, but Russia's war against Ukraine began in 2014. When Ukraine's pro-Russian president Viktor Yanukovych was ousted following mass protests (the Maidan Revolution), Russia annexed Crimea and fomented separatist conflict in eastern Ukraine's Donbas region. A low-grade war killed 14,000 people over eight years before the full invasion.

Vladimir Putin had long argued that Ukraine was not a real country — that it was historically part of Russia, that Russian-speaking Ukrainians were "really" Russian, that NATO expansion toward Russia's borders was an existential threat. In February 2022, he decided to act on these beliefs with military force.

📜 HISTORICAL NOTE

Ukraine was the breadbasket of both the Russian Empire and the Soviet Union. The Holodomor — Stalin's engineered famine of 1932-33 — killed an estimated 3.5 to 7.5 million Ukrainians through deliberately imposed starvation. Ukraine's sense of its own distinct national identity, and its mistrust of Russia, is rooted in this history. When Russians describe Ukrainians as "brothers," Ukrainians sometimes note that this is not how you treat brothers.

CHAPTER ONE

The Failed "Three-Day War"

President Zelensky remaining in Kyiv as Russian forces advanced — when the US offered to evacuate him, his reported reply was "I need ammunition, not a ride." Whether or not he said exactly this, he stayed.

Russia's plan was for a three-day war: advance columns would race to Kyiv, the Ukrainian government would collapse or flee, and a compliant replacement would be installed. Russian troops arrived at Kyiv's outskirts within 48 hours. The plan failed at almost every level.

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky — a former comedian who had won election on an anti-corruption platform — refused to leave. His midnight video addresses from Kyiv streets, showing he was still in the capital while Russian forces were closing in, galvanized Ukrainian resistance and global support. Ukraine's military, restructured and trained with NATO assistance since 2014, fought effectively. Russian logistics collapsed — tanks ran out of fuel, supply convoys were miles long and stuck on roads, communications were poor. Russian forces withdrew from the Kyiv region in late March 2022.

CHAPTER TWO

Attrition, Drones, and the Eastern Front

Mariupol, April 2022 — the port city was besieged and largely destroyed over three months. The Azovstal steel plant became a symbol of Ukrainian resistance as its defenders held out for weeks.

The war shifted east to the Donbas — industrial territory where Russian forces had advantages in numbers, artillery, and supply lines. The fighting resembled WW1 more than any modern conflict: grinding attritional warfare over small amounts of territory, enormous artillery exchanges, trenches and fortifications, massive casualties on both sides. Cities like Bakhmut were reduced to rubble in fighting that lasted months for a few kilometers of ground.

The war also became the world's first large-scale drone conflict. Both sides developed drone warfare rapidly — from commercial consumer drones adapted for dropping grenades, to sophisticated first-person-view (FPV) drones flying kamikaze missions, to long-range strike drones hitting targets hundreds of miles behind lines. Warfare changed in real time, with tactical innovations developed on the battlefield being reverse-engineered and countered within weeks. Every military in the world watched and took notes.

Ukraine's 2023 counteroffensive — anticipated to recapture significant territory — made limited gains against deeply fortified Russian positions. Russia's "meatwave" assault tactics — sending waves of infantry against Ukrainian positions — were horrifically costly but gradually ground forward. North Korean troops arrived to supplement Russian forces. The war settled into a grinding stalemate with no end in clear sight as of early 2025.

CHAPTER THREE

Europe Changed. The World Changed.

Zelensky became Ukraine's Churchill — inspiring resistance, touring Western capitals for aid, refusing all compromise on sovereignty
Vladimir Putin — launched the largest land war in Europe since 1945, betting on quick victory. The bet failed. The war continued.

The war transformed European security overnight. Sweden and Finland — neutral for decades — joined NATO. Germany dramatically increased its defense spending. European unity held far better than Russia expected. Western nations provided Ukraine with over $100 billion in military and economic aid: first anti-tank missiles, then artillery, then tanks, then long-range missiles, then F-16s. Each step was cautious, each step came after months of debate, and each step was eventually taken.

Russia's military, thought to be the world's second most powerful before 2022, revealed significant weaknesses in logistics, equipment maintenance, command and control, and tactical innovation. Casualties were catastrophic. The Russian economy was sanctioned. Hundreds of thousands of Russians fled conscription. The Wagner Group's mercenary commander Yevgeny Prigozhin staged a brief mutiny in June 2023 and was killed in a plane crash two months later. Russia's internal cohesion, though still intact, had visible fractures.

Ukraine suffered enormously: cities bombed, infrastructure destroyed, millions displaced, hundreds of thousands killed. It also demonstrated that a smaller nation, with Western support and extraordinary will, could resist a great power — a lesson absorbed by countries from Taiwan to the Baltics to every autocrat watching from the sidelines.

"I need ammunition, not a ride."
— President Zelensky's reported response when the US offered to evacuate him from Kyiv, February 2022. Whether these exact words were spoken, the sentiment defined his leadership of the resistance.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The Russia-Ukraine War is still unfolding as you read this. Its outcomes will determine whether international law against territorial conquest survives as a norm; whether nuclear threats can be used to deter conventional deterrence; what NATO's future looks like; and whether the post-Cold War order, already stressed, collapses entirely. It is the most important European conflict since 1945. Its history is being written in real time, by people who don't yet know how it ends.
