CHAPTER ONE

Britain's Embarrassing Trade Problem

First Opium War scenes — Britain's most commercially successful and morally indefensible military adventure

By the early 1800s, Britain had a serious problem. The British public had developed a tremendous enthusiasm for Chinese goods: tea, silk, porcelain. China, however, had no equivalent enthusiasm for British goods. The Qing Emperor had told British trade delegations that China "possessed all things in abundance" and required nothing from barbarian nations. This was diplomatically rude but economically accurate.

The result was a massive trade deficit: British silver was flowing into China and not coming back. The East India Company, never short of creative solutions to financial problems, hit upon an answer: opium. Grown in British India, smuggled into China in enormous quantities, sold illegally to Chinese citizens. Chinese silver flowed back to Britain. The British trade deficit became a British trade surplus. Millions of Chinese became addicted. The Qing government considered this a problem.

😤 FUN FACT

When Chinese official Lin Zexu wrote a letter to Queen Victoria asking her to stop the opium trade on moral grounds — pointing out that Britain banned opium domestically while flooding China with it — the British government did not respond. Historians have noted that the letter never actually reached Victoria. The British government was too busy preparing a military expedition.

CHAPTER TWO

Commissioner Lin and the Great Opium Bonfire

In 1839, Chinese Commissioner Lin Zexu seized and destroyed over 20,000 chests of British opium — approximately 1,400 tons of drugs — by mixing it with salt and lime and flushing it into the ocean. He did everything diplomatically correctly. He compensated British merchants with tea. He tried to negotiate. He appealed to morality.

Britain responded by sending warships. The First Opium War (1839–1842) was profoundly one-sided. British steam-powered gunboats could sail up Chinese rivers and bombard cities while Chinese junks couldn't get close. The Treaty of Nanking forced China to pay reparations, cede Hong Kong, and open five "treaty ports" to British trade. It was the first of what China calls the "Unequal Treaties" — a century of humiliations imposed by foreign powers.

The Second Opium War (1856–1860) came when China didn't comply enthusiastically enough. Britain and France together burned the Summer Palace in Beijing — one of the world's great architectural treasures — in deliberate retaliation and to "teach China a lesson." China was forced to legalize opium, open more ports, and grant yet more territorial concessions.

"The wars fought by Britain to enforce the opium trade represent the most shameful events in British history."
— British Prime Minister William Gladstone, 1840. He was outvoted. The war continued.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

China's "Century of Humiliation" (1839–1949) — during which China was repeatedly forced to surrender territory and sovereignty to Western powers and Japan — is still central to Chinese national identity. The Communist Party's legitimacy is partly built on ending this humiliation. Chinese foreign policy, attitudes toward Taiwan and Hong Kong, and negotiations with Western powers are still shaped by these events. The Opium Wars are not ancient history in Beijing.
