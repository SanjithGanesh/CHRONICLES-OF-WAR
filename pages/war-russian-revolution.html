When 300 years of bad Tsars finally catch up to one really unlucky last Tsar

CHAPTER ONE

Russia: A Country-Sized Problem

Tsar Nicholas II — the man who inherited a powder keg and kept lighting matches

Let's set the scene: It's 1917. Russia is absolutely enormous — the largest country on Earth — and it is, by most measurable metrics, a complete disaster.

The peasants (roughly 80% of the population) are starving. The industrial workers in the cities are being ground into the earth by 14-hour workdays for wages that wouldn't impress a Victorian chimney sweep. The middle class is fed up. The military is collapsing. And sitting at the top of this precarious mountain of misery? Tsar Nicholas II — a man so magnificently unsuited to the job that historians still argue whether he was incompetent or just extraordinarily unlucky. (Spoiler: it was both.)

The Romanov dynasty had ruled Russia for 300 years. Three centuries of autocracy, serfdom, and an absolute refusal to share any power with literally anyone. The system was held together by three pillars: the Church (which told people God wanted them to be poor), the Army (which enforced the poverty), and an iron belief that change was not just unwanted but actively sinful.

⚡ FUN FACT

Nicholas II's nickname among his people was "Nicholas the Bloody" — after he approved troops firing on peaceful protesters in 1905. He was, in reality, a gentle family man who loved photography and hated confrontation. History has a cruel sense of irony.

CHAPTER TWO

The Rasputin Problem (Or: When Your Doctor is Also a Cult Leader)

Now, before we get to the revolution itself, we absolutely must discuss Grigori Rasputin — because no history of this era is complete without this extraordinary human being.

Rasputin was a Siberian peasant mystic who somehow became the most powerful man in the Russian court. How? Tsarina Alexandra's son, Alexei, had haemophilia — a condition where blood doesn't clot properly, meaning a small cut could be fatal. Rasputin seemed, inexplicably, to be able to calm the boy during episodes. Whether this was hypnosis, psychosomatic effects, or literal divine intervention, the Tsarina concluded: God sent this filthy peasant monk to save my son, and nobody could tell her otherwise.

By 1916, with Nicholas away at the front losing World War One, Alexandra was effectively running the government. And she was running it by asking Rasputin. Ministers who displeased Rasputin were fired. Policies he liked were adopted. A peasant from Siberia, through a combination of charisma, mysticism, and sheer audacity, was making decisions for the largest country on Earth.

"Rasputin was poisoned, shot, beaten, and thrown into an icy river before he finally died. The official cause of death was drowning. The people who killed him genuinely seemed surprised it took that long."

The Russian nobility, increasingly horrified, conspired to assassinate him in December 1916. The plan: poison him with cyanide-laced wine and cakes. The result: he ate the cakes, drank the wine, and asked for more. Then they shot him. He got up. They shot him again. He ran. They shot him again. Finally, they threw him in the frozen Neva River. He drowned. Even in death, Rasputin was making a statement.

Petrograd, February 1917 — The crowds that told the Tsar it was time to go
CHAPTER THREE

The February Revolution: Russia Fires Its Boss

By February 1917, Russia had been fighting World War One for nearly three years. The results were: 1.7 million Russian soldiers dead, a collapsed economy, and bread shortages in the capital Petrograd. When women textile workers went on strike on International Women's Day (as you do), it triggered a cascade.

Within days, hundreds of thousands of people were in the streets. Nicholas ordered the army to suppress the protests. The army, mostly consisting of cold, hungry, underpaid young men who were also rather tired of the war, largely declined. Whole regiments defected to the protesters.

The Tsar's advisors sent him a simple message: abdicate, or we cannot save you. On March 2, 1917, Nicholas II, last of the Romanovs, ruler of the largest empire on Earth, signed his abdication. He reportedly cried. He reportedly wrote in his diary that evening about the weather. Russia would later add this to the long list of reasons to be frustrated with him.

⚡ TIMELINE NOTE

Russia was still using the Julian calendar in 1917, which ran 13 days behind the Gregorian calendar everyone else used. So the "February Revolution" actually happened in March by everyone else's calendar. The Russians were literally living in the past.

A Provisional Government took over — led by educated, liberal types who believed in democracy and free speech and doing things properly. Noble ideas. Unfortunately, they made one catastrophic decision: they would continue fighting World War One.

This was, to put it gently, deeply unpopular.

📍 The Russian Empire in 1912 — 22.8 million km², 170 million people, zero functioning democracy
CHAPTER FOUR

Lenin Arrives (In a Sealed Train, Like a Biological Weapon)

Vladimir Lenin — professional exile, amateur beard-grower, full-time revolutionary

Enter Vladimir Ilyich Lenin. Lenin had spent years in exile, writing pamphlets about revolution, dreaming of revolution, and being deeply annoyed that no revolution was happening without him.

When the February Revolution broke out, Lenin was in Switzerland. He desperately needed to get to Russia. Unfortunately, Russia was at war with Germany. The Germans, however, were delighted by the prospect of shipping Lenin back to Russia. A revolutionary leader who wanted to end the war? Perfect. They provided him a sealed train.

On April 16, 1917, Lenin arrived at the Finland Station in Petrograd. He stepped off the train and immediately told everyone that the Provisional Government was terrible, the war needed to stop immediately, and that the Bolsheviks (his party) should seize power on behalf of the workers and peasants. His fellow revolutionaries thought this was wildly optimistic. Lenin did not care what his fellow revolutionaries thought.

His program was brilliantly simple: "Peace, Land, Bread." Three words that addressed everything the Russian population had been screaming about for years. The Provisional Government, meanwhile, was offering: "Continue the war, we'll deal with the land thing later, and we're working on the bread."

"Peace, Land, Bread" vs "Wait and See" — history has rarely seen a clearer marketing victory.
CHAPTER FIVE

The October Revolution: The Bolsheviks Steal the Revolution

By October 1917, Russia was in chaos. The Provisional Government had survived one coup attempt (the failed Kornilov Affair), but it was weak, exhausted, and increasingly unpopular. Meanwhile, the Bolsheviks had been patiently arming factory workers and organizing "Red Guards" across Petrograd.

On the night of October 24–25, 1917, Lenin's Bolsheviks made their move. Leon Trotsky coordinated the operation with the Military Revolutionary Committee. Red Guards and sympathetic soldiers began taking over key points in Petrograd: telephone exchanges, railway stations, the state bank, and bridges.

The storming of the Winter Palace — the dramatic centerpiece of Soviet mythology — was, in reality, rather anti-climactic. The building was defended by a few hundred military cadets and a Women's Battalion of Death (yes, that was their actual name). Most of the defenders simply left. When the Bolsheviks finally entered, they reportedly raided the wine cellar and got spectacularly drunk before doing anything official.

The Aurora fired a blank shot — the signal to begin. Even revolutions have stage cues.
The Winter Palace — stormed with considerably less drama than Soviet films suggested

The Provisional Government was arrested. Lenin announced that all power now belonged to the Soviets — councils of workers and soldiers. The Bolsheviks were in charge. The world's first communist state had been born. And now came the hard part: running it.

CHAPTER SIX

Civil War, Red Terror, and the Execution of the Romanovs

The Bolsheviks made peace with Germany in March 1918 (the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk), surrendering vast swathes of territory — a humiliating deal that convinced many Russians that the Bolsheviks were either traitors or madmen. This, combined with their dissolution of the democratically elected parliament (when it produced the wrong result), united a bewildering coalition against them: Tsarists, liberals, social revolutionaries, foreign armies from 14 countries, and people who just really didn't like the Bolsheviks.

The resulting Russian Civil War (1918–1922) was brutal beyond imagination. The "White Army" and "Red Army" fought across a country of 170 million people. Both sides employed terror. The Bolsheviks' Cheka (secret police) shot thousands. White generals massacred villages they suspected of Bolshevik sympathies.

As for the Tsar: the Bolsheviks had been holding Nicholas II and his family under house arrest. In July 1918, with White Army forces approaching the town of Yekaterinburg, local Bolsheviks received an order. In the basement of the Ipatiev House, Nicholas, Alexandra, their five children, and four servants were shot, bayoneted, and — when that proved insufficient — beaten to death. Nicholas II, who had inherited an empire and hoped to leave it better than he found it, ended his dynasty in a basement at 2am, still apparently not sure what had gone so wrong.

📌 AFTERMATH

After three years of brutal civil war, the Red Army won. In 1922, the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (USSR) was officially formed. Lenin died in 1924. A man named Joseph Stalin, who had seemed relatively minor throughout all of this, quietly accumulated enough power to take control. And that, as they say, is another story — and a significantly worse one.

EPILOGUE

Why This Still Matters

The Russian Revolution reshaped the entire 20th century. It created the Soviet Union — a superpower that would define the Cold War for seventy years. It demonstrated that communist revolution was possible, inspiring movements across Asia, Africa, and Latin America. It terrified Western governments into making social reforms they'd previously rejected (nothing motivates a capitalist like the possibility of a workers' uprising).

And it replaced one form of authoritarian rule with another — something the idealistic revolutionaries of 1917 could not have imagined, and something Lenin himself arguably did not intend. The gap between revolutionary ideals and revolutionary reality would define the Soviet experience for the next seventy years.

The Romanovs were officially recognized as martyrs by the Russian Orthodox Church in 2000. The city of Petrograd became Leningrad in 1924, and then St. Petersburg again in 1991 — history's most expensive game of rename-the-city. The Ipatiev House was demolished by Boris Yeltsin in 1977, before anyone knew he'd end up running the country. On the same site today stands the Church on the Blood. Russia, like all countries with complicated histories, is still arguing about what all of it means.
