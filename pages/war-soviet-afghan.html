The Soviet-Afghan War

The war that broke the Soviet Union — and accidentally created the conditions for 9/11. The graveyard of empires struck again.

CHAPTER ONE

The Graveyard of Empires

Soviet soldiers in Afghanistan — young men from the USSR sent to fight in terrain they didn't know, against enemies they didn't understand, for reasons that changed constantly

Afghanistan had defeated every empire that tried to control it. Alexander the Great conquered it briefly. The British tried twice in the 19th century and failed both times memorably. History should have been a warning. History is rarely consulted by people confident in their own military power.

In December 1979, Soviet special forces landed in Kabul, assassinated Afghan President Hafizullah Amin (who had been awkwardly requesting Soviet assistance), installed a more pliable communist leader, and began deploying 80,000 troops. The plan was to stabilize the communist government, train the Afghan army, and withdraw within 6 months. They stayed for ten years.

🎯 BLOWBACK 101

The CIA's Operation Cyclone funneled approximately $3 billion to Afghan mujahideen fighters — the largest covert operation in CIA history. Among the foreign fighters who came to join the jihad against the Soviets was a young Saudi millionaire named Osama bin Laden. America armed and funded networks that would, twelve years after the Soviets left, attack the United States. Foreign policy has long time horizons.

CHAPTER TWO

The Mujahideen and the CIA's War

Afghan Mujahideen fighters — armed with American Stinger missiles, they shot down Soviet helicopters and made the war unwinnable

The Soviet military found itself fighting a classic insurgency in impossible terrain. Afghans knew every mountain path. They melted into the population. They refused to fight in the open. The Soviets responded with brutal tactics — bombing villages, mining farmland, destroying irrigation systems. Five million Afghans fled to Pakistan and Iran — one of the largest refugee crises in history at that time.

The United States, seeing an opportunity to give the Soviets their own Vietnam, began funneling weapons to the mujahideen through Pakistan's intelligence service. The game-changer was the Stinger missile — a shoulder-fired anti-aircraft weapon that could shoot down Soviet helicopter gunships. Before Stingers, Soviet helicopters dominated the battlefield. After Stingers, flying became dangerous. The war that the Soviets had thought would be a police action became a drain on lives, resources, and morale.

CHAPTER THREE

Withdrawal and the USSR's Collapse

By the mid-1980s, under Mikhail Gorbachev, Soviet leadership acknowledged privately that the war was unwinnable. The last Soviet troops crossed the Friendship Bridge out of Afghanistan on February 15, 1989. 15,000 Soviet soldiers had died. A million Afghans were dead. The country was devastated.

The Soviet withdrawal was a significant contributor to the collapse of Soviet prestige and morale that helped bring down the USSR itself in 1991. Afghanistan, having expelled the Soviets, then descended into civil war among the mujahideen factions. From that civil war emerged the Taliban — providing sanctuary to Osama bin Laden's al-Qaeda, which planned and executed the September 11 attacks in 2001. America then invaded Afghanistan — beginning yet another cycle.

"Afghanistan is the place where empires go to die."
— Various historians, journalists, and generals, cited repeatedly from the 19th century onward. The evidence remains consistent.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The Soviet-Afghan War is the most consequential conflict most people have never studied. It weakened and ultimately ended the Soviet Union. It created the jihadist networks that produced 9/11. It established the pattern of superpower failure in Afghanistan that America repeated from 2001-2021. The war that the CIA called "the bleeding wound of the Soviet Union" opened wounds that bled for forty years.
