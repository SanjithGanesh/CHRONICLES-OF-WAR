CHAPTER ONE

A Peninsula Divided by a Line Drawn in 30 Minutes

The Korean War — the front lines swept almost the entire length of the peninsula before ending almost exactly where they started

Korea's division was decided in August 1945 by two American colonels who had never been to Korea, consulting a map in 30 minutes. They drew a line at the 38th parallel to divide Soviet and American occupation zones. North of the line: Soviet-backed communism. South: American-backed democracy. The Koreans were not consulted.

By 1950, the North was armed with Soviet weapons, led by the Soviet-trained Kim Il-sung, and harboring serious ambitions. On June 25, 1950, North Korean forces invaded the South in a massive assault that nearly overran the entire peninsula within weeks. The South Korean and small American forces were pushed into a tiny defensive perimeter around Pusan. It looked like the war might be over before it properly started.

🇺🇳 FUN FACT

The Korean War was technically fought under the United Nations flag — the first major military action authorized by the UN. This was only possible because the Soviet Union was boycotting the Security Council over China's seat and wasn't present to veto the resolution. The Soviets returned to the Council shortly after. This particular accident of timing has never been repeated.

CHAPTER TWO

MacArthur's Gamble and China's Entrance

General Douglas MacArthur — brilliant strategist, spectacular ego, eventually fired by Truman for insubordination. He gave a famous farewell speech. "Old soldiers never die, they just fade away." He faded away to considerable fanfare.
American troops in Korea — fighting in terrain and cold that soldiers weren't prepared for

General Douglas MacArthur responded with a brilliant amphibious landing at Inchon — deep behind North Korean lines — that cut the North Korean army in two. Within weeks, UN forces had pushed not just back to the 38th parallel, but all the way to the Chinese border at the Yalu River.

China, watching an American army reach its border, responded exactly as any great power would: it intervened massively. In November 1950, 200,000 Chinese troops crossed the Yalu and struck the UN forces hard. The Americans were pushed back in one of the most brutal retreats of the 20th century — fighting through freezing temperatures, surrounded, taking catastrophic casualties.

MacArthur wanted to bomb Chinese territory, blockade China, potentially use nuclear weapons. President Truman said no. MacArthur went to the press with his objections — directly contradicting the Commander-in-Chief. Truman fired him. MacArthur returned home to a ticker-tape parade and a famous farewell speech. Truman's approval rating collapsed. The principle of civilian control of the military had been reaffirmed at significant political cost.

CHAPTER THREE

Three Years for a Stalemate

The war settled into a grinding, bloody stalemate near the 38th parallel — exactly where it had started. Two years of armistice negotiations, punctuated by continued fighting, finally produced a ceasefire on July 27, 1953. Not a peace treaty — an armistice. Technically, North and South Korea are still at war. The Korean Demilitarized Zone remains the most heavily militarized border on Earth.

The Korean War killed approximately 3 million people — soldiers and civilians — including 36,000 Americans and 600,000 Chinese. North Korea was the most heavily bombed country in history up to that point: the US dropped more bombs on North Korea than on the entire Pacific theater in WW2. And yet the front ended almost exactly where it started. The North Korean state that emerged from the war became the most isolated, repressive regime on Earth — a condition that persists today under Kim Jong-un.

"Old soldiers never die; they just fade away."
— General Douglas MacArthur, farewell address to Congress, April 1951, after being fired by President Truman.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The Korean War's armistice left a divided peninsula with one of history's most dramatic divergences: South Korea became a democracy and economic powerhouse; North Korea became the world's most isolated state under a hereditary dictatorship. The same people, the same culture, divided by a line drawn in 30 minutes in 1945, produced radically different societies. The Korean peninsula is the world's clearest natural experiment in what political systems do to populations.
