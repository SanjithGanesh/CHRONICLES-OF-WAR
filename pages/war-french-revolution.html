CHAPTER ONE

France in 1789: The Perfect Recipe for Catastrophe

King Louis XVI — a decent, well-meaning man placed in charge of an impossible situation. History was not kind.

In 1789, France was a country with a very large population, a very large debt, and an absolutely terrible system of taxation. The clergy paid nothing. The nobility paid almost nothing. Everyone else paid everything. The peasants and middle classes — the Third Estate — bore essentially the entire financial burden of a state that was technically bankrupt after decades of expensive wars.

Meanwhile, King Louis XVI — a kind, intelligent man with poor political instincts — kept trying to raise taxes on the nobility. The nobility kept blocking him. Louis kept convening and dismissing parliaments. The treasury kept emptying. Then the harvest of 1788 failed, bread prices spiked, and ordinary Parisians couldn't afford to eat.

You can survive bad politics. You can survive bad economics. You cannot simultaneously survive bad politics, economic collapse, bankruptcy, and mass starvation. France tried anyway. It did not go well.

🍞 FUN FACT

The famous quote "Let them eat cake," attributed to Marie Antoinette, was almost certainly never said by her. It appears in Rousseau's writings from a decade earlier. Marie Antoinette was actually more engaged with the poor than her reputation suggests. She was still guillotined, but for different reasons. Fake quotes have been ruining reputations since before the internet.

CHAPTER TWO

The Bastille Falls and Everything Goes Sideways

The Storming of the Bastille, July 14, 1789 — the fortress held exactly 7 prisoners at the time, but symbolism matters more than logistics

On July 14, 1789, Parisian crowds stormed the Bastille — a royal fortress and prison. They found seven prisoners inside, which was somewhat anticlimactic, but the symbolism was enormous. The royal fortress had fallen. The king's authority had crumbled. The revolution had begun.

The National Assembly declared the Declaration of the Rights of Man. In October, thousands of Parisian women marched to Versailles, dragged the royal family back to Paris, and effectively put them under house arrest. The king tried to flee in 1791, was recognized by his own face on a coin, caught at Varennes, and brought back in humiliation. His credibility never recovered.

Meanwhile, the revolution was eating itself. Moderate revolutionaries were outflanked by radical ones. The Jacobins, led by the brilliant and terrifying Maximilien Robespierre, seized power. The king was put on trial. The verdict was never in doubt. On January 21, 1793, Louis XVI was guillotined in the Place de la Révolution before an enormous crowd.

Maximilien Robespierre — architect of the Terror. Also guillotined eventually. Revolutions are circular like that.
Marie Antoinette — guillotined in October 1793, nine months after her husband. She reportedly apologized to her executioner for stepping on his foot. This is either grace or dark comedy.
CHAPTER THREE

The Reign of Terror: Liberté, with Extra Steps

From 1793 to 1794, France descended into what historians call the Reign of Terror. Robespierre's Committee of Public Safety executed anyone suspected of counter-revolutionary sentiment. In ten months, approximately 17,000 people were officially executed and perhaps 40,000 more died in prison or without trial.

The logic of the Terror was circular: anyone who questioned it was clearly counter-revolutionary. Anyone who showed insufficient revolutionary enthusiasm was suspicious. Everyone watched everyone else. Old friends denounced each other. The revolution consumed its own architects, one by one.

It ended, as it had to, when Robespierre himself was arrested and guillotined in July 1794 — the day known as Thermidor. He had tried to arrest members of the Committee who had decided, preemptively, to arrest him first. The morning he was seized, his jaw had been shattered in an apparent suicide attempt. He was guillotined bandaged, in pain, before the same crowd that had watched so many others die. The audience reportedly cheered.

⚡ FUN FACT

The guillotine was considered a humane form of execution — quick, equal regardless of social class, and relatively painless. Its inventor, Dr. Guillotin, spent the rest of his life appalled by its association with mass killing. His family later changed their surname to escape the association. The machine outlasted the family's embarrassment: France kept using it until 1977.

CHAPTER FOUR

The Revolutionary Wars: France vs. Everyone

Napoleon crossing the Alps — painted gloriously. The reality involved a mule rather than a rearing horse, but the propaganda version looked better.

While France was guillotining its own citizens, the rest of Europe was panicking. The neighboring monarchies — Austria, Prussia, Britain, Spain, Russia — could see what was happening and were terrified the ideas would spread. They formed coalitions and invaded France to restore the monarchy.

France, galvanized by the invasion, found something remarkable: revolutionary armies fight differently. French citizens weren't fighting for a king or for pay — they were fighting for ideas, for the nation, for the republic. They fought with ferocity that professional soldiers couldn't match. France won defensive battle after battle, then began pushing back, then began conquering.

Into this chaos rose a young artillery officer from Corsica: Napoleon Bonaparte. He won brilliant victories in Italy in 1796–97, conquered Egypt (briefly), and returned to Paris to find a collapsing government desperate for a strongman. In November 1799, Napoleon staged a coup — the 18 Brumaire — dissolved the revolutionary government, and made himself First Consul. The revolution was over. The empire was about to begin.

"The revolution was like Saturn, it devoured its own children."
— Jacques Mallet du Pan, Swiss journalist. One of the few people watching from outside who understood what was happening.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The French Revolution introduced ideas that permanently changed the world: national sovereignty, the secular state, civil equality under law, mass conscription, and the concept that governments derive legitimacy from the people. It also introduced the concepts of "left" and "right" in politics — the two factions literally sat on different sides of the National Assembly chamber. Every political argument since 1789 uses this vocabulary.
