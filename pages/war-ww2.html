PROLOGUE

How to Create a World War: A Recipe in Several Catastrophic Steps

World War Two did not begin on September 1, 1939. It had been building for twenty years, one terrible decision at a time. If you want to understand WW2, you have to understand WW1 — specifically, you have to understand the Treaty of Versailles, which ended WW1 by humiliating Germany so thoroughly that it practically came with a twenty-year timer labeled "Next War: Insert Grievance Here."

Germany was forced to: accept sole blame for WW1 (the "War Guilt Clause"), pay enormous reparations it could not afford, surrender territory, drastically reduce its military, and have its economy strangled. The result was runaway inflation so severe that people burned banknotes for warmth because the wood was cheaper, followed by the Great Depression, followed by mass unemployment, followed by a population that was exhausted, humiliated, and looking for someone to blame.

Into this vacuum stepped a failed Austrian art student with a talent for public speaking, an extraordinary gift for identifying and exploiting resentment, and a worldview so horrifying that most people — right up until it was too late — simply refused to believe he meant it literally.

CHAPTER ONE

The Rise of Hitler: When Nobody Took the Manifesto Seriously

Adolf Hitler — failed artist, successful autocrat, architect of the 20th century's worst catastrophe

Adolf Hitler wrote Mein Kampf in prison in 1924. In it, he laid out, in extensive and not-very-subtle detail, exactly what he intended to do: destroy democracy, eliminate Jewish people, conquer Eastern Europe, and create a racially "pure" German empire. The book sold millions of copies. Most people, apparently, thought it was hyperbole.

By 1933, Hitler was Chancellor of Germany. By 1934, he was Führer — absolute dictator. He moved with astonishing speed: the Reichstag fire gave him emergency powers; he eliminated political opponents; he began rearming Germany in violation of Versailles; and with each step, the Western democracies made a calculation: appease him and maybe he'll stop.

This strategy was called Appeasement. Its most famous moment came in September 1938 at Munich, when Britain's Neville Chamberlain agreed to let Hitler have the Sudetenland (the German-speaking part of Czechoslovakia) in exchange for a promise of no further territorial demands. Chamberlain returned to Britain waving a piece of paper and declaring "peace for our time." Hitler got Czechoslovakia by March 1939.

"Chamberlain had a choice between war and dishonour. He chose dishonour. He will have war." — Winston Churchill, who was not wrong.
The Wehrmacht — the most modern army in the world in 1939, built from nothing in six years
CHAPTER TWO

Blitzkrieg: When War Became Terrifyingly Fast

On September 1, 1939, Germany invaded Poland. Britain and France, who had guaranteed Polish independence, declared war. World War Two had officially begun.

The German military had developed a new form of warfare: Blitzkrieg — "lightning war." Instead of the slow, grinding attritional warfare of WW1, Blitzkrieg used fast-moving armored columns supported by dive bombers to punch through enemy lines, encircle defenders, and move so quickly that the enemy never had time to respond. Poland fell in 27 days. France, which had survived four years of WW1, fell in six weeks.

The fall of France in June 1940 was one of the most stunning military defeats in modern history. The French had more tanks than Germany. Their defences were theoretically sound. They simply couldn't respond to the pace and direction of the German attack. 330,000 British and French soldiers were evacuated from the beaches of Dunkirk by a fleet of civilian boats — described at the time as a miracle, which it was, though miracles usually don't leave you in quite such desperate circumstances.

📌 THE MIRACLE OF DUNKIRK

The evacuation of Dunkirk saved the British Expeditionary Force, but the soldiers had to abandon virtually all their equipment on the beaches — 84,000 vehicles, 2,500 guns, and 400,000 tons of supplies. Britain was left with enough weapons to equip roughly one division. Churchill called it "a colossal military disaster." He was correct. He also called it "a miracle of deliverance." He was also correct about that.

CHAPTER THREE

Britain Alone, and the Battle of Britain

After France fell, Britain stood essentially alone. Hitler expected Britain to negotiate — he actually admired the British Empire and had no particular desire to destroy it. Churchill, who had replaced the disgraced Chamberlain as Prime Minister, declined to negotiate in language so memorably combative that it still makes excellent quote magnets.

Without a peace agreement, Hitler had to invade Britain or bomb it into submission. He chose bombing. From July to October 1940, the German Luftwaffe and the Royal Air Force fought the Battle of Britain — the first major campaign fought entirely by air forces. British fighter pilots (average age: 20) flew multiple sorties a day against a numerically superior enemy. The RAF lost nearly 1,000 aircraft. The Luftwaffe lost nearly 2,000.

Hitler gave up. He turned his attention east. This was his second catastrophic strategic error, and in one sense it saved Western civilization: by invading the Soviet Union on June 22, 1941 — under Operation Barbarossa — Hitler opened a two-front war that Germany could not win.

CHAPTER FOUR

Operation Barbarossa: Hitler Invades Russia (Again, This Fails)

Germany invaded the Soviet Union with 3.8 million soldiers — the largest invasion force in history. Initial results were staggering: entire Soviet armies were encircled and destroyed; by December 1941, German forces were within sight of Moscow.

Then winter arrived. Then the Soviets recovered. Then Stalingrad happened.

The Battle of Stalingrad (August 1942 – February 1943) became the turning point of the entire war. Hitler ordered Stalingrad taken; Stalin ordered it held at any cost. What followed was five months of the most brutal urban combat in history — fighting building by building, room by room, sometimes floor by floor. A sniper's war in the ruins of a city. German and Soviet soldiers died at a rate of approximately 6,000 per day.

In November 1942, the Soviets launched Operation Uranus — a masterpiece of encirclement that trapped the entire German 6th Army (330,000 men) inside Stalingrad. Hitler refused to let them break out. On February 2, 1943, the remnants of the 6th Army — 91,000 men, including 24 generals — surrendered. After Stalingrad, Germany never won a major offensive on the Eastern Front again.

"At Stalingrad, the war turned. Not with a battle, but with an entire army ceasing to exist."
CHAPTER FIVE

Pearl Harbor: Japan Wakes Up America

Meanwhile, in the Pacific, Japan had been fighting its own war in China since 1937 — a conflict of appalling brutality involving the systematic massacre of civilians and POWs. The United States had been supplying China and imposing economic sanctions on Japan. Japan's military decided the solution was to eliminate the American Pacific Fleet before it could intervene.

On December 7, 1941, Japanese aircraft struck the American naval base at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii. 2,403 Americans died. Eight battleships were sunk or damaged. It was a tactical success and a strategic catastrophe: it destroyed the American battleship fleet while leaving aircraft carriers at sea, and it united a previously divided American public absolutely against Japan.

America declared war on Japan. Germany, in a moment of exceptional strategic miscalculation, declared war on America in support of its Japanese ally. The industrial might of the United States — which would produce more aircraft, tanks, and ships than the rest of the world combined — entered the war. Germany and Japan had, between them, chosen to fight the world's largest economies simultaneously. The outcome, in retrospect, was not in doubt. The cost to get there was extraordinary.

CHAPTER SIX

D-Day: The Largest Seaborne Invasion in History

On June 6, 1944, the Allied forces launched Operation Overlord — the invasion of Normandy. 156,000 soldiers crossed the English Channel on the largest seaborne invasion force ever assembled. Five beaches. Five nations. Years of preparation. One day that changed history.

The losses were horrific, particularly at Omaha Beach, where American troops faced devastating German fire and nearly 2,000 casualties. But by nightfall, the Allies had established a foothold in France. By August, Paris was liberated. By December, Germany itself was being invaded from west and east simultaneously.

CHAPTER SEVEN

The Holocaust: History's Greatest Crime

No story of WW2 can omit the Holocaust. The systematic murder of six million Jewish people — along with Roma, disabled people, homosexuals, political opponents, and others — was not an accidental byproduct of war. It was a deliberate, industrialized genocide carried out by the Nazi state with explicit orders, detailed records, and the participation of thousands of ordinary people at every level.

The Nazis built a network of concentration camps, extermination camps, and mobile killing units that operated across occupied Europe. Auschwitz-Birkenau alone killed over 1.1 million people. The victims were transported from across Europe, often believing they were being "resettled." The reality was designed to defy imagination.

This was not medieval barbarity. It was planned, bureaucratized, modern industrial murder — carried out by a literate, sophisticated, culturally advanced nation at the height of the 20th century. The Holocaust is studied not because it was an aberration from human nature, but because it was an expression of what human nature is capable of, under specific conditions, in a specific political environment. The conditions that created it are not unique to Germany.

📌 REMEMBER

The Holocaust was not discovered at the war's end — it was known about in considerable detail by Allied governments as early as 1942. The decision not to prioritize its interruption (by bombing rail lines to the camps, for example) remains one of history's most contested moral failures.

CHAPTER EIGHT

The End: Atomic Bombs and a New World

Germany surrendered on May 8, 1945. Hitler had shot himself in his Berlin bunker as Soviet troops closed in. The Thousand Year Reich had lasted twelve years and left Europe in ruins.

Japan continued fighting. American planners estimated an invasion of Japan could cost up to a million casualties. President Truman authorized a new weapon: the atomic bomb, developed under the Manhattan Project by a team that included the greatest physicists alive. On August 6, 1945, an atomic bomb destroyed Hiroshima. On August 9, a second destroyed Nagasaki. Between 130,000 and 230,000 people died, mostly civilians. Japan surrendered on September 2, 1945.

The world that emerged from WW2 was fundamentally different from the one that entered it. Europe, which had dominated the world for centuries, was in ruins and deeply in debt to America. The United States and Soviet Union were now superpowers — and their relationship would define the next fifty years. The United Nations was created. Israel was founded. NATO was formed. Nuclear weapons existed. Decolonization began.

And the world began the long, difficult work of trying to understand how it had happened — and how to make sure it never happened again. We are still working on that.
