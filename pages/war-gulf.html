CHAPTER ONE

Saddam Hussein Misreads the Room — Catastrophically

Saddam Hussein — Iraqi dictator who invaded Kuwait in 1990, apparently convinced the world would not respond. He was wrong in the most consequential possible way.

In August 1990, Iraqi President Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait — a small, oil-rich emirate on Iraq's southern border. His grievances were real but his timing was catastrophic: the Cold War had just ended, the Soviet Union was collapsing, and America under George H.W. Bush was looking for a way to demonstrate the "New World Order."

Saddam had several reasons to think the world might accept the invasion. Kuwait had been pumping oil above its OPEC quota, depressing prices and hurting Iraq's post-Iran War finances. Iraq had historical claims to Kuwait. And the American ambassador, April Glaspie, had told Saddam in a meeting days before the invasion that America had "no opinion" on Arab border disputes — a statement Saddam apparently took as permission. It was not permission. The US assembled a 34-nation coalition in six months.

🛢️ OIL CONTEXT

Kuwait and Iraq together held about 20% of the world's known oil reserves. Saudi Arabia, watching Iraqi forces mass on its border, was terrified. When King Fahd asked for American protection, US Secretary of Defense Dick Cheney showed him satellite photos of Iraqi troops near the Saudi border. Saudi Arabia agreed to host American forces. This was the first large deployment of US troops to the Arabian Peninsula — a decision with consequences nobody fully anticipated.

CHAPTER TWO

Operation Desert Storm: The 100-Hour War

Coalition forces in the Gulf — the most powerful military force assembled since World War II, deployed in the desert to liberate a country in 100 hours

After a 38-day air campaign that systematically dismantled Iraqi air defenses, communications, and supply lines, the ground war began on February 24, 1991. General Norman Schwarzkopf executed a massive flanking maneuver — the "left hook" — swinging hundreds of thousands of troops around the Iraqi right flank through what Iraq thought was impassable desert.

The result was not a war but a rout. Iraqi forces, many of whom had been under air bombardment for weeks without supplies, surrendered in masses — sometimes to journalists, sometimes to unmanned drones. The much-feared Republican Guard, Iraq's elite troops, was surrounded and shattered. Kuwait City was liberated on February 26. Saddam Hussein called a ceasefire. The ground war lasted 100 hours.

America's military performance was stunning. Precision-guided weapons, night-vision equipment, and satellite communications gave coalition forces overwhelming technological advantages. Casualties were astonishingly low: 147 Americans killed in combat. Iraqi military dead numbered in the tens of thousands. The Vietnam syndrome — the conviction that America couldn't win wars — evaporated in the desert sand.

Saddam Hussein's retreating forces set fire to 700 Kuwaiti oil wells — burning for eight months, creating the worst environmental disaster in history at that point
CHAPTER THREE

Why Bush Stopped at Baghdad (And Why That Mattered)

President Bush made the decision to stop the war rather than advance to Baghdad and remove Saddam from power. The reasons were strategic: the UN mandate only authorized liberating Kuwait, not regime change. Advancing to Baghdad would have required occupying Iraq — an Arab nation — with an army that included Arab partners who would never have supported that objective. The coalition would have fractured. And then what? Occupy Iraq? Install a government? The question of what comes next had no good answer.

Saddam Hussein survived. He immediately crushed Kurdish and Shia uprisings that the US had encouraged. America watched and did nothing. The Kurds fled to the mountains; the Shia were massacred. For a decade, Iraq was kept contained by sanctions, no-fly zones, and occasional airstrikes. Saddam's weapons programs continued. The question of what to do about him was left for another administration, another crisis, another war.

That war came in 2003. The question of "what comes next" after removing Saddam got its answer, and the answer was catastrophic.

"We're not going to baby-sit a civil war."
— Colin Powell, on why the US stopped short of Baghdad in 1991. The phrase would prove grimly prophetic about what happened when a later administration did not stop short.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The Gulf War established American military dominance in the post-Cold War world and created the template for "precision warfare." It also stationed American troops permanently in Saudi Arabia — which Osama bin Laden cited as a primary grievance motivating the 9/11 attacks. The war that was so decisively won planted seeds that grew into the 2003 Iraq War, the rise of al-Qaeda, and two decades of Middle Eastern conflict. Victory is rarely the end of the story.
