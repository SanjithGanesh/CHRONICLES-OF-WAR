The Vietnam War

CHAPTER ONE

France Fails First (Then America's Turn)

Ho Chi Minh — Vietnamese revolutionary who spent decades fighting first France, then Japan, then France again, then America. He died before the war ended but his side won.

Vietnam had been a French colony since the 1880s. The Vietnamese had resisted French rule for decades. When Japan occupied Vietnam during WW2 and France couldn't protect its colony, the writing was on the wall. Ho Chi Minh — a communist who had studied Marx in France and helped found the Comintern — led the Viet Minh independence movement, quoting the American Declaration of Independence in his own independence declaration in 1945.

France tried to re-impose colonial rule anyway. Eight years of guerrilla war followed. It ended in 1954 at Dien Bien Phu, where 13,000 French troops were surrounded and besieged by Viet Minh forces who had dragged artillery through jungle mountains that the French thought impassable. France surrendered. Vietnam was divided at the 17th parallel. America, already funding France's war, stepped in to prevent the North from reunifying the country under communism.

📜 FUN FACT

Ho Chi Minh wrote to President Truman eight times asking for American support for Vietnamese independence based on the Atlantic Charter's principles of self-determination. He received no reply. The US was busy funding France's colonial war instead. Ho Chi Minh turned to the Soviet Union and China. The domino the US was trying to prevent was partially tipped by the US ignoring its own stated principles.

CHAPTER TWO

The Domino Theory and the Escalation Trap

American forces in Vietnam — fighting in jungle terrain, against an enemy that faded into the civilian population and knew every inch of the country

American involvement escalated through three presidencies, each trapped by the logic of the previous commitment. Eisenhower sent advisers. Kennedy sent more advisers. Johnson, after the ambiguous Gulf of Tonkin incident — where North Vietnamese patrol boats may or may not have attacked US destroyers — used a congressional resolution to commit full military force.

By 1968, 500,000 American troops were in Vietnam. The US military was killing enemy fighters at a rate that, mathematically, should have won the war. The strategy was called "attrition" — kill enough of them and they'll give up. What American strategists didn't fully grasp was that North Vietnam and the Viet Cong were not fighting to win militarily. They were fighting to outlast American willingness to sustain casualties.

The Tet Offensive of January 1968 changed everything. 80,000 North Vietnamese and Viet Cong fighters simultaneously attacked over 100 cities and towns across South Vietnam — including the US Embassy in Saigon. The attack was militarily defeated within weeks. But Americans watching on television saw burning cities and their embassy under attack, while their government had been telling them the war was being won. Trust collapsed.

CHAPTER THREE

Protests, My Lai, and the Collapse of Consensus

The Tet Offensive, 1968 — the attack that won the propaganda war even as it lost the military one

Back home, America was tearing itself apart. The anti-war movement grew from campus protests to mass marches of hundreds of thousands. In May 1970, National Guard soldiers shot four students at Kent State University during a protest. America was at war with itself over the war.

The My Lai Massacre — where US soldiers killed between 347 and 504 unarmed Vietnamese civilians in 1968, reported months later — shattered the moral certainty with which the US had entered the war. Lieutenant Calley was convicted. The Army covered it up for a year. The photos of the dead — including children — ran in every newspaper in the world.

Nixon was elected promising to end the war. He secretly expanded it into Cambodia and Laos while withdrawing American troops — "Vietnamization," the strategy of having South Vietnamese fight their own war. The Pentagon Papers, leaked in 1971, revealed that the government had systematically lied to the public about the war's progress for years. Trust in government plummeted to levels not seen before or since.

CHAPTER FOUR

The Last Helicopter Out of Saigon

The evacuation of Saigon, April 1975 — the defining image of American defeat, a helicopter on the roof of the US Embassy as South Vietnam fell

America signed the Paris Peace Accords in 1973 and withdrew its forces. Two years later, North Vietnamese tanks rolled into Saigon. On April 30, 1975, the last Americans were evacuated by helicopter from the roof of the US Embassy as South Vietnamese people desperate to leave fought to board. The images — helicopters, rooftops, desperate crowds — became the defining image of American defeat.

Vietnam was reunified under communist rule. The country that had defeated the world's greatest military power had lost approximately 3.8 million people — combatants and civilians across both sides. Two million are still affected by Agent Orange, the defoliant the US sprayed across millions of acres of Vietnamese jungle. The war that America "lost" left scars on two nations simultaneously.

"How do you ask a man to be the last man to die for a mistake?"
— John Kerry, Vietnam veteran and future Secretary of State, testifying before the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, 1971.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

Vietnam permanently changed how America conducts war. The all-volunteer military replaced the draft. The "Vietnam Syndrome" — public reluctance to commit ground troops to foreign conflicts — shaped every military intervention for decades. The war also produced a generation of cynical, anti-establishment American voters who've never fully trusted government since. And ironically, Vietnam today is a thriving market economy with close economic ties to the United States. History's sense of irony is merciless.
