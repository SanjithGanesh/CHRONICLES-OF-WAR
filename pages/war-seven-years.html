PROLOGUE

Before There Were World Wars, There Was This

Most people, when they think of "World Wars," think of 1914 or 1939. They picture trenches, tanks, and people wearing helmets. They do not picture men in powdered wigs fighting on four separate continents simultaneously over who gets to be the most powerful country on Earth.

Which is a shame, because that is exactly what the Seven Years' War was — and it was genuinely the first conflict in history to involve major military operations on multiple continents at the same time. Winston Churchill called it "the first world war." Historians largely agree. It just happened to occur about 150 years too early for anyone to name it dramatically.

🌍 THE SCALE

The Seven Years' War was fought in Europe, North America, South America, West Africa, India, and the Philippines. It involved Britain, France, Prussia, Austria, Russia, Spain, Portugal, and basically anyone else with a flag and a grievance. For context: most wars have two sides. This one had everyone.

CHAPTER ONE

The Diplomatic Revolution: Everyone Switches Sides

Frederick the Great of Prussia — brilliant general, terrible neighbor, deeply unpleasant person

The war started because of something historians actually call the "Diplomatic Revolution of 1756" — which sounds dry but was, in practice, absolutely chaotic. Europe's great powers suddenly switched their alliances around in ways that confused everyone, including their own diplomats.

Here's how it worked: Austria and France had been enemies for centuries. Britain and Prussia had been friendly. Then, in 1756, everyone swapped. Austria allied with France. Britain allied with Prussia. The result was two enormous blocs staring at each other across Europe while simultaneously fighting each other's colonies across the entire globe.

At the center of this storm stood Frederick II of Prussia — known to history as Frederick the Great, known to his enemies as "that infuriating little kingdom that won't stop winning." Frederick was surrounded on all sides by Austria, France, Russia, Sweden, and Saxony. He had Prussia, some excellent generals, and what appeared to be suicidal confidence. Somehow, he survived.

Empress Maria Theresa — wanted Silesia back badly enough to rearrange all of European diplomacy
Louis XV of France — allied with Austria, then lost most of his empire anyway
CHAPTER TWO

The American Front: Where Britain Really Won

The Plains of Abraham, Quebec, 1759 — where French Canada effectively ended in about 15 minutes

In North America, this war was called the French and Indian War, which Americans know far better than the Seven Years' War because they name conflicts after who they fought rather than how long they lasted. Here, Britain and France were fighting over who would control the vast interior of the continent.

France had a significant advantage at first — they'd built a network of forts and allied with many Native American nations. Then Britain sent massive reinforcements, installed William Pitt the Elder as war minister, and started spending money like it was going out of fashion. The turning point came in 1759, the "Year of Victories," when Britain won at Quebec, at Lagos, at Minden, and in India — all within months. The French were getting absolutely battered on every front simultaneously.

The Battle of the Plains of Abraham outside Quebec lasted approximately fifteen minutes, but its consequences lasted centuries. Both commanding generals — Wolfe for Britain, Montcalm for France — were killed. Britain won. French Canada, after 150 years of French rule, became British. French Canadians are still a little annoyed about this.

🦅 FUN FACT

A young officer named George Washington fought in the early stages of this war as a British colonial officer — and performed so badly in his first military engagement that he accidentally started an international incident. He would later lead a revolution against Britain. Which is awkward.

CHAPTER THREE

India and Africa: The Colonial Jackpot

Robert Clive — East India Company employee who accidentally conquered India. This is considered normal behaviour in the 18th century.

Meanwhile, in India, another titanic struggle was taking place. The British East India Company — technically a trading company, practically a private army with stockholders — was fighting the French East India Company for control of the subcontinent. Both sides allied with local rulers, bribed generals, and behaved in ways that were technically legal but profoundly questionable.

Robert Clive — a clerk-turned-general with extremely flexible ethics — won the decisive Battle of Plassey in 1757 through a combination of military skill and outright bribery of the enemy commander. This single battle effectively handed Britain the foundation of what would become its Indian empire. Clive was made very rich. The Bengali people were not consulted.

In West Africa, Britain captured French slave-trading posts. In the Caribbean, Britain took French islands. The pattern was consistent: France won in Europe occasionally, lost almost everywhere else.

CHAPTER FOUR

Prussia Survives (Barely)

Battle of Kunersdorf, 1759 — Prussia's worst defeat. Frederick wrote his own obituary after this one.

Back in Europe, Frederick of Prussia was fighting for his life — sometimes literally. In 1759, his army was shattered at the Battle of Kunersdorf. He wrote letters suggesting Prussia was finished. He reportedly considered suicide. Vienna prepared celebrations.

Then something extraordinary happened: Empress Elizabeth of Russia died in 1762. Her successor, Peter III, was a colossal admirer of Frederick the Great — so instead of delivering the killing blow to Prussia, Russia switched sides and returned all conquered territory. Historians call this the "Miracle of the House of Brandenburg." Frederick called it the luckiest moment of his life. Russia immediately replaced Peter III with his wife, Catherine — who would become Catherine the Great — but kept the peace.

Prussia survived. Frederick went on to rule for another two decades. Austria never got Silesia back. Everyone was exhausted.

EPILOGUE

The Treaty of Paris (1763): Britain Wins Everything

When the shooting stopped, the Treaty of Paris (1763) redrew the world. France handed over Canada to Britain, most of its Indian territories, and various Caribbean and African possessions. Spain, which had joined France late and badly, gave up Florida. Britain emerged as the dominant global power — in control of North America east of the Mississippi, India, and much of the global trade network.

The losers were also notable: France lost its empire and accumulated enormous debt trying. Spain lost territory. Most significantly, Native American nations across North America lost French allies who had helped balance British expansion — and within a generation, British colonists were pouring across the Appalachians with predictable consequences.

🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The Seven Years' War's debt forced Britain to tax its American colonies — sparking the resentments that led to the American Revolution thirteen years later. It also weakened France financially to the point where, decades later, they had their own revolution. One war, two revolutions, both consequences. The butterfly effect was working overtime in 1763.
