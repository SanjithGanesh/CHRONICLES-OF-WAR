🌙 COLD WAR · 1954–1962

The Algerian War

France's bloodiest decolonization: 132 years of colonial rule, 8 years of brutal war, and the question that tore French society apart

CHAPTER ONE

132 Years of French Algeria

France had occupied Algeria since 1830 — longer than it had occupied any other territory. Over 130 years, over a million French settlers (pieds-noirs) had made Algeria their home. France treated Algeria not as a colony but as an integral part of France itself — three departments on the Mediterranean, as French as Normandy. The 9 million Arab and Berber Algerians who had been there before the French arrived had rather different views on this arrangement.

On November 1, 1954, the Front de Libération Nationale (FLN) launched coordinated attacks across Algeria — blowing up bridges, attacking French military posts, assassinating a teacher. The Algerian War had begun. France, fresh from defeat in Vietnam, was not in the mood to negotiate. It sent the army.

⚖️ HISTORICAL NOTE

The Sétif massacre of May 1945 — where French security forces and settlers killed between 6,000 and 45,000 Algerian Muslims following an independence demonstration — is cited by Algerians as the real beginning of the independence movement. It took France until 2005 to describe Sétif as a "tragedy" and until 2021 for Emmanuel Macron to acknowledge it as a "crime against humanity." The numbers are still disputed.

CHAPTER TWO

The Battle of Algiers and the Torture Debate

Algerian women played crucial roles in the independence movement — carrying messages, concealing weapons, sometimes carrying bombs. The FLN deliberately used women because French soldiers were less likely to search them.

In 1957, the FLN launched a bombing campaign in Algiers. The French military, under General Jacques Massu, responded with systematic torture and collective punishment. The Battle of Algiers succeeded militarily — the FLN network was destroyed — but at catastrophic moral cost. French paratroopers tortured thousands. Some were "disappeared" — dropped into the sea from aircraft.

The torture scandal divided France. Intellectuals, priests, military officers, and politicians spoke out. Jean-Paul Sartre and Simone de Beauvoir signed petitions. The French left was appalled. The French military and most settlers were defiant. The war became the central political question of French society — and it wasn't just about Algeria.

In 1958, the crisis brought down the Fourth Republic entirely. General Charles de Gaulle returned from retirement as France's political system collapsed. He had promised to keep Algeria French. He then spent four years carefully, methodically reversing that position — negotiating Algerian independence while surviving multiple assassination attempts by outraged settlers and military officers.

CHAPTER THREE

Independence and the Harkis

The Évian Accords of March 1962 gave Algeria independence. Within months, about 900,000 pieds-noirs — French settlers whose families had been in Algeria for generations — fled to France in one of history's largest sudden migrations. They arrived in a country that couldn't accommodate them and which regarded them with complex guilt.

The fate of the Harkis — Algerian Muslims who had fought for France — was catastrophic. Abandoned by the French army, they were massacred by the thousands by FLN forces in the post-independence violence. Estimates range from 30,000 to 150,000 killed. Those who made it to France were interned in camps. France only formally apologized for abandoning the Harkis in 2021.

"France without Algeria would be like a body without a head."
— French Prime Minister Guy Mollet, 1956. Six years later, France had made Algeria independent and gone on to thrive without it. The analogy was poor.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The Algerian War permanently shaped France: its relationship with immigration (Algeria sent workers to rebuild post-war France), its Muslim population (France has Europe's largest, mostly of North African origin), its debates about national identity, torture, and colonial memory. The FLN became the ruling party of Algeria — and is still in power today, which says something about revolutions and the tendency of liberation movements to become the governments they replaced.
