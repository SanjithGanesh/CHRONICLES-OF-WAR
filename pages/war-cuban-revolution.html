CHAPTER ONE

Batista's Cuba and the Young Lawyer with a Plan

Fidel Castro in 1959 — the bearded rebel who outlasted 10 US presidents and held power for 49 years. Not bad for a man who attacked a barracks with 165 people.
Ernesto "Che" Guevara — Argentine doctor, revolutionary, icon. Later executed in Bolivia trying to start another revolution. His face became the most reproduced image in 20th century poster art.

In 1953, Cuba was run by dictator Fulgencio Batista — corrupt, brutal, and enthusiastically backed by the United States. American companies owned large portions of Cuba's land, casinos, and economy. Ordinary Cubans were poor. Batista's secret police tortured dissidents. The mafia ran Havana's casinos.

Into this picture stepped Fidel Castro — a 26-year-old lawyer who had been radicalized at university and decided that revolutionary violence was the answer. On July 26, 1953, he led 165 rebels in an assault on the Moncada Barracks in Santiago. It failed completely. Half his men were killed or captured. Castro was captured, tried, and sentenced to 15 years in prison. In his closing statement to the court he declared: "Condemn me, it does not matter. History will absolve me." It became his most famous sentence.

CHAPTER TWO

The Sierra Maestra and the Guerrilla Campaign

The Sierra Maestra mountains — where Castro's guerrillas hid, trained, and built the movement that toppled Batista from a force of 20 men

Batista, in a spectacular political miscalculation, released Castro in 1955 as a goodwill gesture. Castro immediately went to Mexico, recruited more revolutionaries including an Argentine doctor named Ernesto "Che" Guevara, and returned to Cuba in December 1956 on a leaking yacht called the Granma with 82 men.

They landed in the wrong place, were immediately attacked by Batista's forces, and scattered. About 20 survived to reach the Sierra Maestra mountains. From this improbable starting point, they rebuilt. Guerrilla warfare, peasant support, and spectacular propaganda — including an interview with New York Times journalist Herbert Matthews that made Castro famous worldwide — grew their force from 20 to thousands over two years.

By late 1958, Batista's demoralized army was collapsing. On January 1, 1959, Batista fled Cuba. Castro's forces entered Havana. The revolution was complete.

CHAPTER THREE

Bay of Pigs to Missile Crisis: 90 Miles from Catastrophe

Bay of Pigs invasion, April 1961 — CIA-trained Cuban exiles, disastrously planned, without US air cover, captured on the beach. Kennedy inherited the plan and owned the failure.

Castro's revolution turned communist. The US, appalled to find a Soviet ally 90 miles from Florida, organized a CIA-trained army of Cuban exiles to invade and overthrow him. The Bay of Pigs invasion of April 1961 was one of the most embarrassing failures in CIA history — the invasion force was captured on the beach, the promised American air support never came, and Kennedy had to ransom 1,200 prisoners for $53 million worth of food and medicine.

Then came October 1962 — the Cuban Missile Crisis. Soviet missiles in Cuba. American naval blockade. Thirteen days during which the world was closer to nuclear war than at any other time in history. Kennedy and Khrushchev negotiated back from the brink. The Soviets removed their missiles. America secretly promised not to invade Cuba and removed its missiles from Turkey. Castro, who was not consulted about any of this, was furious.

"History will absolve me."
— Fidel Castro, at his trial in 1953 after the failed Moncada attack. He was right about the history, somewhat debatable about the absolution.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The Cuban Revolution established a communist state 90 miles from the US, creating the defining Cold War tension of the Western Hemisphere. The Missile Crisis remains the closest humanity has come to nuclear war. Cuba's experience also exported revolution: Che Guevara's attempts to spread the Cuban model to Congo and Bolivia ended in his capture and execution in 1967. His face became the most recognizable political image of the 20th century — on T-shirts worn by people who know almost nothing about what he actually did.
