⚜ 19TH CENTURY · 1870–1871

The Franco-Prussian War

The war that accidentally created Germany, humiliated France, and set Europe on the path to World War I — all in under a year

CHAPTER ONE

Bismarck's Masterplan

Otto von Bismarck — Chancellor of Prussia, architect of German unification, and possibly the most calculated political strategist in European history

Otto von Bismarck was the kind of man who played chess while everyone else was playing checkers, and who set the board on fire when chess wasn't working. As Chancellor of Prussia, his goal was simple: unify the German states under Prussian leadership into a single German nation. To do this, he needed a war — specifically, a war against France that would force the other German states to rally behind Prussia.

The pretext was almost absurdly thin. The Spanish throne was vacant. A German prince was a candidate. France objected — a Hohenzollern on the Spanish throne would mean Prussia on two borders. The Prussian king agreed to withdraw the candidate. France, pushing its luck, demanded a written guarantee that Prussia would never propose such a candidate again. The Prussian king sent Bismarck a telegram describing the exchange. Bismarck edited it to make both sides sound ruder than they were, leaked it to the press, and watched outrage ignite on both sides. France declared war. The trap had snapped shut.

✂️ FUN FACT

Bismarck's edit of the "Ems Dispatch" is history's most consequential piece of political spin-doctoring. He made a polite telegram sound like mutual insults, triggering two nations into war. When asked about this, he essentially shrugged and noted that war was going to happen anyway — he just scheduled it for the most convenient time. This is the sort of candour that makes historians nervous.

CHAPTER TWO

France Collapses in Six Weeks

Napoleon III surrendering to the Prussians at Sedan — the French Emperor captured in the field, which hadn't happened since... ever, really

The Prussian military was shockingly effective. Organized by Helmuth von Moltke, it used railroads, superior artillery, and synchronized corps movements that French commanders had never encountered. French forces were outmaneuvered, surrounded, and beaten before they could concentrate.

The catastrophe came at Sedan in September 1870. The main French army of 100,000 was surrounded. Napoleon III — nephew of the great Napoleon, consistently compared to him unfavorably — was captured personally. The French Emperor of the French was a Prussian prisoner. The Second French Empire immediately collapsed. A new republic was declared in Paris that same afternoon.

The war wasn't over — France kept fighting — but the outcome was. Paris was besieged for four months. Parisians ate rats, horses, and eventually the animals from the zoo. The city held out, achieved nothing, and surrendered in January 1871.

CHAPTER THREE

Germany Is Born (In France's Palace)

Proclamation of the German Empire in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles, January 18, 1871 — a location chosen for maximum humiliation of France. Bismarck was thorough.

In a move of breathtaking diplomatic rudeness, Bismarck proclaimed the new German Empire in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles — France's most magnificent palace, currently occupied by Prussian troops. Wilhelm I was proclaimed German Kaiser. The German state that had not existed a year earlier was now the most powerful nation on the European continent.

France's humiliation was total. The Treaty of Frankfurt stripped France of Alsace and Lorraine — wealthy industrial provinces — and imposed an enormous indemnity. French politicians signed with tears in their eyes. French schoolchildren learned about Alsace-Lorraine for the next forty years. When the next war came, France would not forget.

"We have won too much. Our victory will cost us dearly."
— German Crown Prince Frederick after the war. He was right. The consequences arrived in 1914.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The Franco-Prussian War created the strategic environment for World War I. A unified, powerful Germany unsettled European balance of power. French desire to recover Alsace-Lorraine made France permanently hostile to Germany. The humiliation at Versailles meant France would insist on humiliating Germany in return in 1919 — which set up 1939. One war, threaded through history like a wire connecting three conflicts across 75 years.
