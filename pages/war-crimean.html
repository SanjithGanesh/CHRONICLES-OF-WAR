CHAPTER ONE

The "Sick Man of Europe" and Who Gets to Inherit Him

Tsar Nicholas I of Russia — called the Ottoman Empire "the sick man of Europe" and immediately started planning who should inherit the estate

By the 1850s, the Ottoman Empire — once the terror of Europe — was visibly crumbling. Tsar Nicholas I of Russia famously called it "the sick man of Europe" and started making plans for what would happen when it died. His plans involved Russia acquiring Constantinople and warm-water ports on the Mediterranean, which would make Russia a dominant naval power.

Britain and France had strong opinions about this. Specifically: no. An all-powerful Russia with Mediterranean access was not in their interests. When Russia invaded Ottoman territories in present-day Romania in 1853, Britain and France declared war and sent forces to defend the Ottomans — less out of affection for Turkey and more out of profound dislike of Russian expansion.

🤔 FUN FACT

The war was technically triggered by a dispute over which Christian group — Catholic or Orthodox — had the right to protect holy sites in Jerusalem. The Ottomans gave certain privileges to French Catholics. Russia demanded the same for Orthodox Christians. France refused to let Russia have equal status. This religious dispute spiralled into a major European war. Over church keys, essentially.

CHAPTER TWO

The Charge of the Light Brigade: A Masterclass in What Not to Do

The Charge of the Light Brigade — 600 cavalry charged directly into Russian artillery due to a miscommunicated order. It was, in the words of a French general present: "magnificent, but not war."

The war is best remembered for two things: catastrophic military incompetence and Florence Nightingale. Let us cover the incompetence first.

On October 25, 1854, at the Battle of Balaclava, British cavalry received a garbled order to advance. Due to confusion about which guns they were targeting, the Light Brigade — 600 mounted soldiers — charged directly toward the main Russian artillery battery. They rode through a valley with Russian guns on three sides. Two hundred were killed or wounded. They took the guns briefly, then withdrew. Nothing was accomplished.

The poet Tennyson immortalized it. Military historians use it as a case study in communication failure. The soldiers who died in the valley had no idea why they were charging. Their officers had no idea why the order had been given. The general who gave the order thought he was targeting a different set of guns entirely. Nobody was court-martialled.

CHAPTER THREE

Florence Nightingale: The Lady with the Lamp (and the Statistics)

Florence Nightingale — nurse, statistician, healthcare reformer. Invented modern nursing and modern data visualization simultaneously. An overachiever.

More soldiers died of disease in the Crimean War than of battle wounds. British military hospitals were filthy, understaffed, and dangerous. Florence Nightingale arrived at Scutari hospital in 1854 and found conditions she described as worse than anything she'd imagined — sewage running under the wards, rats, inadequate food, no basic supplies.

Nightingale and her nurses cleaned the wards, organized supplies, and implemented basic sanitation. The death rate at Scutari dropped from 42% to 2% within six months. She also invented the modern polar area chart — a type of statistical diagram — to demonstrate to Parliament why soldiers were dying and what needed to change. She was simultaneously a nurse and a pioneering data scientist. History has mostly remembered the lamp.

"The very first requirement in a hospital is that it should do the sick no harm."
— Florence Nightingale. Revolutionary at the time. Still occasionally revolutionary now.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The Crimean War was the first war extensively covered by embedded journalists and photographers. The horrors reported back to British readers created public pressure for military reform. It led directly to the professionalization of nursing and modern military medicine. It also left Russia humiliated and resentful — motivating the reforms of Alexander II and the chain of events that would eventually culminate in 1917.
