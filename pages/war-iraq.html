PROLOGUE

September 11 Changed Everything — Including Things That Had Nothing to Do with It

September 11, 2001 — Al-Qaeda's attack killed 2,977 people and launched two decades of American military engagement in the Middle East

On September 11, 2001, nineteen hijackers — fifteen from Saudi Arabia, two from the UAE, one from Egypt, one from Lebanon — crashed four aircraft, killing 2,977 people in New York, Virginia, and Pennsylvania. The attack was planned by al-Qaeda, based in Afghanistan, led by Osama bin Laden.

America invaded Afghanistan in October 2001 to destroy al-Qaeda and remove the Taliban government that sheltered it. This was broadly supported internationally as legitimate self-defense. Then, in a fateful pivot, the Bush administration turned its attention to Iraq — which had not been involved in September 11, which had no operational links to al-Qaeda, and which had not attacked the United States. The stated reason was weapons of mass destruction. The real reasons were more complicated.

🔍 STATED VS ACTUAL

The Iraq War's stated rationale shifted repeatedly: WMDs, links to al-Qaeda, regime change, democratization, regional stability. Colin Powell's presentation to the UN Security Council in February 2003 — which he later called "a permanent blot on my record" — cited intelligence about mobile biological weapons labs and aluminum tubes. Both claims were wrong. The intelligence was either fabricated, misinterpreted, or presented with far more certainty than the underlying evidence warranted.

CHAPTER ONE

Shock and Awe: Three Weeks to Baghdad

US Marines entering Baghdad, April 2003 — the conventional war took three weeks. The occupation lasted eight years and is still ongoing in various forms.

The conventional war was brief and decisive. Beginning on March 20, 2003, American and British forces executed "Shock and Awe" — a massive air and ground campaign designed to collapse Iraqi resistance through overwhelming psychological impact. It worked. Baghdad fell on April 9, 2003. The famous toppling of Saddam's statue in Firdos Square was broadcast worldwide — a stage-managed scene that showed perhaps 200 people, mostly journalists, surrounded by US military vehicles.

President George W. Bush landed on USS Abraham Lincoln on May 1, 2003, beneath a banner reading "Mission Accomplished." This was, to put it charitably, premature. The mission that had been accomplished — removing Saddam — created a power vacuum that unleashed forces no one in the Bush administration had adequately planned for.

CHAPTER TWO

Disbanding the Army: The Decision That Unleashed Chaos

Saddam Hussein at trial — captured December 2003, tried and hanged December 2006. Removing him proved far easier than managing what came after.
Abu Ghraib prison abuse photos — released in 2004, they destroyed American moral authority in the region and became recruitment tools for insurgents worldwide

The Coalition Provisional Authority made two decisions that historians now regard as catastrophic. First: de-Baathification — removing all members of Saddam's Baath party from government positions. Second: disbanding the Iraqi Army. This put 400,000 armed, trained, unemployed, humiliated men on the street with no income and no future.

They became the insurgency. Former Iraqi military officers — including those who would later lead ISIS — organized the resistance. The insurgency that the US military had not planned for exploded: car bombs, IEDs, ambushes, sectarian violence between Sunni and Shia that American planners had dismissed. Abu Ghraib — where American soldiers photographed themselves torturing and humiliating Iraqi prisoners — destroyed American moral authority in the region and became the most effective recruitment video in al-Qaeda's history.

CHAPTER THREE

The Surge, ISIS, and the Unending Aftermath

American soldiers in Iraq — 4,431 Americans died, along with over 100,000 Iraqi civilians in the eight years of occupation

By 2006, Iraq was in full sectarian civil war. American casualties were mounting. Public support had evaporated. President Bush, in a politically courageous decision that contradicted public and military opinion, ordered the "Surge" — deploying an additional 30,000 troops under General David Petraeus with a new counterinsurgency strategy focused on protecting civilians. Violence dropped dramatically. The surge succeeded tactically.

American combat forces withdrew in 2011. Within three years, ISIS — the Islamic State, led largely by former Iraqi Army officers and powered by years of Sunni grievance — had seized a third of Iraq and declared a caliphate. America sent troops back. The war it had declared over in 2011 was not over. It never fully ended.

Total costs: 4,431 American military dead; between 150,000 and 1 million Iraqi civilian deaths (estimates vary widely); approximately $2 trillion spent. The weapons of mass destruction were never found. An official inquiry concluded the intelligence used to justify the war was wrong.

"Stuff happens."
— Donald Rumsfeld, Secretary of Defense, responding to reports of looting and chaos in Baghdad, April 2003. The quote became emblematic of the administration's failure to plan for occupation.
🔗 WHY IT MATTERS

The Iraq War destroyed America's credibility as a builder of democracy, gave Iran enormous strategic gains by eliminating its primary enemy, created ISIS, exhausted the American military, polarized domestic politics, and contributed to the "America First" backlash against interventionism. It also showed that winning a war militarily and winning the peace are completely different problems — and that failing to plan for the second can erase all gains from the first. Every war since has been conducted in the shadow of Iraq.
